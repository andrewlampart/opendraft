## Paper 2: Game Localization
**Authors:** O'Hagan, Minako; Mangiron, Carme
**Year:** 2013
**Venue:** John Benjamins Publishing (Benjamins Translation Library)
**DOI:** 10.1075/btl.106
**Citations:** High Impact (Foundational Monograph)

### Research Question
How can game localization be conceptualized within Translation Studies, considering the specific technological and cultural constraints of the digital entertainment industry? The authors aim to bridge the gap between "Game Studies" and "Translation Studies" to provide a roadmap for professional practice and academic research.

### Methodology
- **Design:** Comprehensive Academic Monograph / Theoretical Framework.
- **Approach:** Interdisciplinary review combining Translation Studies (TS) theories with Ludology (Game Studies).
- **Data:** Case studies of industry practices, analysis of "transcreation" workflows, and historical progression of game hardware constraints.

### Key Findings
1. **Redefinition of Translation:** Game localization moves beyond linguistic transfer to "transcreation"—where the "playability" and "fun factor" prioritize over semantic accuracy.
2. **Technological Determination:** The medium (hardware, memory, UI limitations) dictates the translation strategy more than traditional text types; "technologization" is central to the translator's role.
3. **GILT Framework:** Establishes the hierarchy of Globalization, Internationalization, Localization, and Translation, placing translation as a sub-component of a larger industrial process.

### Implications
This is a seminal text that legitimized game localization as a sub-discipline of Translation Studies. It provides the theoretical vocabulary (transcreation, playability) used by almost all subsequent researchers (including Papers 6, 8, and 10 in this list). Practical application involves training translators to be "localizers" who understand code variables and UI constraints.

### Limitations
- **Age:** Published in 2013; predates the massive explosion of Live Service Games (GaaS) and AI-driven localization workflows.
- **Scope:** Primarily focuses on the console/PC paradigm of that era, potentially under-addressing the mobile-first ecosystem seen in later papers (like Paper 6).

### Notable Citations
- **Bernal-Merino (Paper 1):** Cited regarding the pedagogical needs for training translators.
- **Mateas & Stern (Paper 11):** Likely referenced regarding the intersection of narrative and gameplay mechanics.
